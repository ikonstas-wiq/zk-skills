# Common Corrections and Attribution Guidelines

## CRITICAL: Evidence Classification

### Two Types of Evidence Requiring Different Handling

**Type 1: DIRECT PARTICIPATION**
- Person was present in meeting/conversation AND spoke
- Evidence includes what they actually said
- Observable behaviours in situations they attended
- **Handling:** Can be direct and specific in feedback

**Type 2: DISCUSSED ABOUT**
- Others discussed the person without them present
- Leadership conversations about their work or development
- Second-hand observations or assessments
- **Handling:** Must heavily soften and generalize—EXTREME CARE required

### The Risk of DISCUSSED Evidence

Using discussed evidence without proper softening risks:
- Revealing that leadership talks about people behind their back
- Creating perception of political environment
- Damaging trust and psychological safety
- Making person feel exposed or criticized rather than supported

**Golden rule:** Would the person reading this feel they're being developed, or would they feel they're being talked about? If the latter, soften further or mark [TO REVIEW].

## Attribution Rules for Multi-Speaker Meetings

When processing meeting transcripts with multiple participants, **ONLY attribute statements to the person who actually said them**. Meeting transcripts clearly mark speakers (e.g., "Michelle:", "Zac:", "Harrison:").

### What You CAN Do:
- Cite direct quotes from the person
- Reference their questions, proposals, and stated positions
- Note their observable behaviours in meetings they attended
- Use what they actually said as evidence

### What You CANNOT Do:
- Infer what someone thinks based on being present in a meeting
- Attribute specific observations about a team member to the team member themselves
- Claim someone said something when it was discussion about them by others
- Convert "the group discussed X's time management" into "you have time management issues"

### Example:

**Meeting transcript shows:**
```
Leader: "Person X books long meetings and accepts too many invitations"
Person X: [present but didn't speak on this topic]
```

**WRONG attribution:**
"You book long meetings and accept too many invitations" (Charlotte didn't say this, Michelle said it about her)

**CORRECT handling:**
"There's an opportunity to be more strategic about meeting load and selective engagement" (softened, no attribution, developmental framing)

## Softening Framework for DISCUSSED Evidence

### Development Feedback Transformation

When development feedback comes from leadership discussions:

**Step 1: Extract the theme** (not the criticism)
- What's the actual developmental area?
- Strip away judgmental language

**Step 2: Reframe as opportunity**
- "doesn't do X" → "developing capability in X would..."
- "poor at Y" → "opportunity to strengthen Y"
- "needs to Z" → "building Z capability could enhance..."

**Step 3: Add context and support**
- Why would developing this help them?
- What would success look like?
- Frame as natural growth edge, not performance gap

**Step 4: Remove all attribution**
- No hints about who said what
- No reference to discussion context
- Pure forward-looking guidance

**Step 5: Apply trust test**
- Would they feel supported or exposed?
- If exposed, soften further or mark [TO REVIEW]

### Transformation Examples

**Example 1: Time Management**

Source (from leadership discussion):
"Charlotte books long meetings, accepts too many invitations, works from 6am to compensate which masks the problem"

❌ TOO DIRECT:
"Your time management is poor. You book long meetings and accept too many invitations, using early morning work to compensate."

✅ APPROPRIATELY SOFTENED:
"There's a development opportunity around strategic attention allocation. Consider whether your current meeting load enables deep focus on your 2-3 key priorities. Your early morning work demonstrates strong commitment, and pairing that with more selective meeting acceptance could amplify your impact on areas where you're building ownership."

**Example 2: Critical Questioning**

Source (from leadership discussion):
"She treats stakeholder statements as gospel, doesn't form independent opinion, says yes to everything"

❌ TOO DIRECT:
"You treat stakeholder requirements as gospel rather than forming your own opinion. You say yes to everything instead of questioning whether requests make sense."

✅ APPROPRIATELY SOFTENED:
"As you develop product leadership capability, there's an opportunity to strengthen critical questioning of requirements. Building confidence to respectfully probe stakeholder requests—asking whether they align with strategic direction and represent optimal use of capacity—would enhance effectiveness in product roles. This isn't about being oppositional, but rather developing independent judgment about what should versus could be built."

**Example 3: Scope Management**

Source (from leadership discussion):
"She loves being across everything, never says no, can't be lead if you agree to everything"

❌ TOO DIRECT:
"You love being across everything and never say no. You can't be a lead if you agree to everything every stakeholder says."

✅ APPROPRIATELY SOFTENED:
"There's an opportunity to focus your impact. As you develop toward lead roles, strengthening your ability to hold boundaries on scope—being selective about what you take on versus what might be better owned by others—would increase your effectiveness. The principle that people remember one thing done exceptionally well rather than ten things touched lightly could guide prioritisation decisions."

## Forbidden Attribution Language

### NEVER Use These Phrases:

**Third-party observation language:**
- "Leadership observed..."
- "Leadership noted..."
- "Leadership has identified..."
- "Leadership discussed..."
- "It was observed that..."
- "Feedback indicated..."
- "Others have noted..."
- "The team noticed..."
- "Colleagues mentioned..."
- "In discussions about you..."
- "Feedback from others suggests..."

**Why forbidden:** These phrases explicitly reveal that people talked about the person, damaging trust and creating political perception.

### ALWAYS Use These Patterns:

**Direct observational framing:**
- "There's an opportunity to..."
- "Consider whether..."
- "As you develop X..."
- "Building capability in Y would..."
- "Strengthening Z could enhance..."
- "In discussions, you..." (only if they were present)
- "You've demonstrated..." (factual observation)
- "The pattern noted is..." (if genuinely observable)
